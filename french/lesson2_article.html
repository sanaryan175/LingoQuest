Lesson 2: Numbers in French

Learn how to count and use French numbers in everyday situations – with clear meanings and pronunciations!

🔢 Numbers from 0 to 20

  • 0 – Zéro (zay-roh)
  • 1 – Un (uh[n])
  • 2 – Deux (duh)
  • 3 – Trois (trwah)
  • 4 – Quatre (katr)
  • 5 – Cinq (sa[n]k)
  • 6 – Six (sees)
  • 7 – Sept (set)
  • 8 – Huit (weet)
  • 9 – Neuf (nuhf)
  • 10 – Dix (dees)
  • 11 – Onze (onz)
  • 12 – Douze (dooz)
  • 13 – Treize (trez)
  • 14 – Quatorze (ka-tohrz)
  • 15 – Quinze (kanz)
  • 16 – Seize (sez)
  • 17 – Dix-sept (dees-set)
  • 18 – Dix-huit (dees-weet)
  • 19 – Dix-neuf (dees-nuhf)
  • 20 – Vingt (va[n])

🎯 Milestone Numbers

  • 30 – Trente (tront)
  • 40 – Quarante (ka-ra[n]t)
  • 50 – Cinquante (sa[n]-ka[n]t)
  • 60 – Soixante (swah-sont)
  • 100 – Cent (so[n])
  • 1000 – Mille (meel)
  • 10,000 – Dix mille (dees meel)

💡 Grammar & Number Patterns

  • 💬 In French, numbers above 16 start to combine: Dix-sept = 17 ("Ten-seven").
  • 🧩 French uses hyphens often: Vingt-et-un = 21 ("Twenty-and-one").
  • 📌 Unlike English, the word for "and" appears only in some numbers like 21, 31, 41... not in others.
  • 💯 For hundreds and thousands, just add: Cent deux = 102, Mille cinq = 1005.

📘 Usage in Context

J'ai quinze ans.
I am 15 years old.

Il coûte trente euros.
It costs 30 euros.

Nous avons cent élèves.
We have 100 students.

Elle habite au numéro vingt-et-un.
She lives at number 21.

La chaîne a dix mille abonnés.
The channel has 10,000 subscribers.

🧠 Did You Know?

  • 🔄 In French, numbers often mix smaller numbers. Example: Dix-sept = 17 (10+7)
  • 🧮 The French word for "80" is literally "4 twenties" — quatre-vingts (4×20 = 80!)
  • 🎧 Numbers like 99 are long: quatre-vingt-dix-neuf = 99 (“4x20 + 10 + 9”)
  • 🗣️ Pronounce numbers often! Native speakers say them quickly, so practice slowly and clearly first.
  • 🔢 Once you learn 1–20 and multiples of 10, you can build most numbers easily!
